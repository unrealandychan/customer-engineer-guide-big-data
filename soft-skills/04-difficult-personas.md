# 👥 Difficult Personas — Reading the Room & Adapting

> **The single most important skill in a room:** Reading who you're actually talking to.  
> The same pitch that wins a CTO will lose a CFO.  
> The same energy that connects with a champion will alienate a skeptic.  
> Adapt or lose.

---

## The 8 Archetypes

---

### Archetype 1: The Skeptic

**How to recognize them:**
- Arms crossed, leaning back
- Asks "but what about..." after every point
- Doesn't make eye contact, or stares too hard
- Gives short, clipped responses: "Okay." "And?" "We'll see."
- Often the most technically experienced person in the room

**What drives them:**  
They've been burned before — by a vendor, by a failed migration, by hype that didn't deliver. They're protecting their team.

**What NOT to do:**
- ❌ Try to convince them with more features
- ❌ Get defensive when they push back
- ❌ Talk over their objections

**How to adapt:**
> *"I can tell you're not someone who takes things at face value — I actually appreciate that. What would it take to make a believer out of you? I'd rather know your test than fail a test I didn't know I was taking."*

Let them define the success criteria. Then meet it. Skeptics who convert become your loudest champions.

**Power move:** Give them something to find wrong with.  
> *"Here's a limitation of this approach that I want to flag upfront..."*  
Proactively naming a weakness disarms the skeptic and builds trust faster than a flawless pitch.

---

### Archetype 2: The Deep Technical Expert (The Nerd)

**How to recognize them:**
- Asks very specific questions ("What's the P99 latency under slot contention?")
- Corrects your terminology
- Has a laptop open and is fact-checking you in real time
- Doesn't care about business impact — only technical correctness
- May derail the conversation with deep-dives

**What drives them:**  
They need to know the system will work. They're the ones who'll have to implement it and live with it.

**What NOT to do:**
- ❌ Brush off detailed technical questions
- ❌ Give vague answers ("it's very fast")
- ❌ Try to redirect to business value when they want to go deep

**How to adapt:**
- Meet them at their level. If you know the answer, go technical.
- If you don't know: *"That's the exact right question — and I want to get you the precise answer rather than approximate. Let me follow up today."*
- Give them a technical resource: whitepaper, benchmark study, GitHub repo.
- Invite them to be the expert: *"You probably know this environment better than anyone in the room. What would you design differently?"*

**Managing their tangents:**
> *"This is a great thread — I want to make sure we get to it fully. Can we park it for 10 minutes and circle back? I don't want the rest of the group to lose context."*

---

### Archetype 3: The CFO / Numbers Person

**How to recognize them:**
- Asks "what does it cost?" within the first 5 minutes
- Eyes glaze over during technical discussion
- Asks "what's the ROI?" before you've explained the product
- Wants one-number summaries: "Just give me the bottom line."

**What drives them:**  
Money in and money out. They care about budget risk, cost predictability, and measurable return.

**What NOT to do:**
- ❌ Talk about features or architecture
- ❌ Give a number without context
- ❌ Use technical acronyms (they feel excluded and disengage)

**How to adapt:**
Translate everything to money:
- "Faster queries" → *"Your analysts get answers in seconds instead of hours — that's 2 hours/day per analyst back, across a 30-person team, that's $X per year in productivity."*
- "Serverless" → *"You eliminate the infrastructure ops budget — that's typically $200K–$400K/year for a team managing on-prem clusters."*
- "Better reliability" → *"A 99.99% SLA means less than 1 hour of downtime per year. What does an hour of data platform downtime cost your business?"*

Frame risk in money terms: *"The risk of not moving is $X in ongoing ops cost + $Y in delayed analytics decisions per quarter."*

---

### Archetype 4: The Silent Evaluator

**How to recognize them:**
- Senior person in the room who says almost nothing
- Observing, not participating
- Others occasionally glance at them for reaction
- May be the actual decision-maker

**What drives them:**  
They're assessing trust, competence, and whether you understand their business. They'll decide after the meeting, not during it.

**What NOT to do:**
- ❌ Ignore them
- ❌ Address all your remarks to the louder people

**How to adapt:**
- Address them directly, at least twice in the session:
  > *"[Name], I'd love your perspective on this — what's your read on the current challenge?"*
- Send your best material to them directly in the follow-up email.
- When closing: *"[Name], is there anything we didn't cover that you'd want to address before we wrap up?"*

The Silent Evaluator often makes the call. Earn their attention.

---

### Archetype 5: The Competitor Fan

**How to recognize them:**
- Mentions a competing product (AWS, Snowflake, Databricks) multiple times
- Phrases things as comparisons: *"In Redshift, this works like X. How does yours work?"*
- May have certification or community ties to the competing platform
- Challenges you to justify differentiation

**What drives them:**  
They've invested time and credibility in the other platform. Switching feels like admitting they were wrong.

**What NOT to do:**
- ❌ Directly attack the competing platform
- ❌ Dismiss their experience with it
- ❌ Get into a feature war

**How to adapt:**
> *"I respect [competitor] — it's a strong platform and your experience with it is real. I'm not here to tell you it's bad. What I want to understand is: where are the friction points? Every platform has them. What's the thing your team wishes was different?"*

The "coexistence frame" is powerful:
> *"In a lot of shops we work with, [competitor] and BigQuery coexist — [competitor] does X, BigQuery does Y. It doesn't have to be either/or."*

---

### Archetype 6: The Overtalker

**How to recognize them:**
- Answers questions with 5-minute monologues
- Goes off-topic constantly
- Takes every thread into a tangent
- Often the most enthusiastic person in the room

**What drives them:**  
They love the topic, want to be heard, and often have important context buried in the noise.

**What NOT to do:**
- ❌ Let them run for more than 3 minutes without redirecting
- ❌ Cut them off rudely

**How to adapt:**
The "Yes And" redirect — validate their point, then redirect:
> *"That's a really important context — [specific acknowledgment]. Based on what you just said, I want to make sure we address [specific element]. Can I redirect us there?"*

The physical redirect: stand up or step toward the whiteboard — movement signals a topic shift.

The time limit frame:
> *"I want to make sure we get through everything today — can I capture that point and we continue in the interest of time?"*

---

### Archetype 7: The Decision-Maker in a Hurry

**How to recognize them:**
- Checks their phone repeatedly
- Says *"Can we move this along?"*
- Wants to skip to the end: *"Just tell me what you recommend."*
- Will leave early if they're not captured in the first 5 minutes

**What drives them:**  
Time. They have 6 other priorities. They need a quick, confident recommendation — not a journey through your logic.

**What NOT to do:**
- ❌ Walk through your full discovery process with them
- ❌ Build up to the answer
- ❌ Give options without a clear recommendation

**How to adapt:**
Lead with the recommendation (Pyramid Principle):
> *"Here's my recommendation: [Answer]. Here's why: [Top 2 reasons]. Here's what it requires: [Key action]. I can walk through the detail if you want it, but that's the bottom line."*

If they have to leave early:
> *"Before you go — can I give you the 60-second version? [Give it.] The detailed material will be in the follow-up. Is there anything you want me to make sure we cover before you have to step out?"*

---

### Archetype 8: The Internal Champion (Under Pressure)

**How to recognize them:**
- Enthusiastic about the solution, but visibly nervous around their manager
- Over-explains your case to the room: *"What they mean is..."*
- May have already committed to recommending you internally
- Under pressure from both sides: they want this to work

**What drives them:**  
They've put their credibility on the line. They need you to make them look smart.

**What NOT to do:**
- ❌ Undermine them in front of their manager (even accidentally)
- ❌ Let them do all the selling for you — they'll exhaust themselves
- ❌ Ignore the political dynamics

**How to adapt:**
Make them the expert in the room:
> *"[Champion's name] — you know this environment better than I do. Did I capture your current challenge accurately?"*

Give them ammunition before the meeting (pre-brief):
> *"Before we go in — what are the top 3 objections you're expecting from your leadership? Let me make sure I address those directly."*

After the meeting, send the follow-up to their manager — CC the champion. It validates them up the chain.

## Quick Persona Cheat Sheet

| Persona | Care About | Speak Their Language |
|---------|-----------|---------------------|
| The Skeptic | Proof, not promises | Evidence, limitations, honesty |
| The Deep Technical | Correctness, depth | Specs, whitepapers, benchmarks |
| The CFO | ROI, cost, risk | $, %, payback period |
| The Silent Evaluator | Competence, trust | Direct questions, respect |
| The Competitor Fan | Not being wrong | Coexistence, fair comparison |
| The Overtalker | Being heard | Acknowledgment, redirection |
| Decision-Maker in a Hurry | Time, clarity | Bottom-line up front |
| The Champion | Their credibility | Validation, ammunition |

## The Mixed Room Problem

Most real meetings have 3–4 different archetypes simultaneously.

**Strategy:**
1. Open with the Silent Evaluator (ask them a direct question) — capture the room's attention by surfacing the most senior voice
2. Address technical depth for The Nerd — shows competence to the whole room
3. Translate to business value for the CFO — shows range
4. Give the Champion a moment to shine
5. Close to the Decision-Maker — give the recommendation clearly

**Multi-persona redirect:**  
> *"[Nerd], that's a great technical point — I want to make sure [CFO's name] has context on why that matters. The business implication of what you just raised is..."*

You're simultaneously validating the Nerd AND translating for the CFO — both feel served.
