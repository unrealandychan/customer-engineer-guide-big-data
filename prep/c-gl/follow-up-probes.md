# Interview C — G&L: Follow-Up Probes

This is your stress-test file. Run through these the night before Interview C.
For every story in `story-inventory.md`, here are the hardest follow-up questions Andy could ask — with your scripted answer structure.

**Rule:** If you can answer all of these without notes, you're ready. If any answer feels thin, strengthen that story first.

---

## Stories 1A + 1B — Leading Without Authority

**Probe 1:** *"What would you have done if they'd never come around to your proposal?"*
→ "I'd have committed to the decision that was made, documented my concerns clearly, and set a review checkpoint 90 days out to reassess with data. Influence without authority means you don't always win — but you stay in the conversation."

**Probe 2:** *"How did others perceive you stepping in when it wasn't your mandate?"*
→ Be honest. Some may have felt it was overstepping. Name that directly: "One team lead initially felt I was encroaching on their domain. I addressed it by leading with listening — I spent 2 weeks learning their constraints before sharing my proposal. They became a co-author of the final recommendation."

**Probe 3:** *"What would you do differently if you started over?"*
→ "I'd map the stakeholders more carefully upfront — who has formal authority, who has informal influence, who's a blocker. I underestimated the informal power dynamics early on and had to course-correct."

**Probe 4:** *"How did you maintain momentum when people were slow to commit?"*
→ "I created low-stakes entry points — a 1-hour working session instead of a committee. Small commitments build toward larger ones. I also made it easy to say yes: I handled the documentation, the deck, the scheduling."

**Probe 5:** *"What leadership trait mattered most in that situation?"*
→ "Patience and listening. The easiest thing would have been to push my proposal from day one. The harder thing — and the thing that worked — was making others feel like they'd shaped the solution."

## Story 2 — Conflict / Pushback

**Probe 1:** *"What if the decision-maker had shut you down completely?"*
→ "I would have committed to the decision in action while documenting my concern in writing. One more escalation path: I'd have asked if there was a checkpoint we could set — e.g., 'Can we revisit this at the 60-day mark with data?' That preserves accountability without undermining authority."

**Probe 2:** *"Did the relationship survive? How is it today?"*
→ [Answer from your real experience. If it's good: show that respectful challenge builds trust. If it was rocky: show what you learned about timing and framing.]

**Probe 3:** *"Was there a moment you doubted yourself?"*
→ "Yes. When I saw how strongly they felt, I wondered if I'd missed something. So before pushing back, I gave myself a forcing function: steelman their argument for 10 minutes. I still concluded they were wrong, but going through that step meant my pushback was much more specific and credible."

**Probe 4:** *"How did you decide when to push back vs when to let it go?"*
→ "The threshold for me is: user/customer impact, data integrity, or team wellbeing. Those I'll push back on once, clearly, with data. Matters of preference or style — I'll share my view and defer."

**Probe 5:** *"What would you do differently?"*
→ "I'd have raised the concern 2 weeks earlier, before the decision was publicly announced. Influencing a decision in progress is much easier than reversing one already made."

## Story 3 — Ethical Pressure

**Probe 1:** *"What if you'd been wrong — and the thing you blocked would have been fine?"*
→ "That's a real risk I accepted. My standard isn't certainty — it's that I could live with the decision if it went wrong in the worst-case scenario. I'd rather be too cautious on user trust than too casual with it."

**Probe 2:** *"How did you protect the relationship with the person applying the pressure?"*
→ "I separated the person from the pressure. I didn't make it about them being wrong — I framed it as me having a constraint I couldn't work around, and proposed alternatives that met their underlying goal without the ethical risk."

**Probe 3:** *"Was this the right hill to die on?"*
→ Own this fully. "Yes. Because the downstream cost of [customer data issue / compliance violation / user harm] would have been far higher than the short-term cost of holding the line."

**Probe 4:** *"How do you decide what's a genuine ethical issue vs just disagreeing with someone?"*
→ "Genuine ethical issues involve user harm, data integrity, honesty, or team wellbeing. If it's just a decision I'd make differently, that's not ethics — that's preference. I'm clear with myself about which category I'm in before I invoke that frame."

**Probe 5:** *"What did this situation cost you professionally?"*
→ [Be honest here. If there was a cost, name it and show you'd do it again. That's the real signal Andy is looking for — that you'll do the right thing even when it costs you something.]

## Story 4 — Learning / Humility

**Probe 1:** *"How did you handle the moment of realizing you were wrong?"*
→ "The first instinct was to explain it away. I caught myself doing that, which was the signal. I gave myself a rule: if I find myself spending more energy explaining a counter-example than evaluating it, I stop and re-examine my assumptions from scratch."

**Probe 2:** *"How did others react when you changed your position publicly?"*
→ "Generally with more trust, not less. Admitting you were wrong is rare enough that it tends to build credibility when done cleanly — without deflection or excessive self-flagellation."

**Probe 3:** *"Do you think you update your views too easily? Or not easily enough?"*
→ This is a trap question — show you've thought about it. "I've been told I can be difficult to move on first principles. On technical details, I update quickly with data. On values questions, I hold my ground longer. I think that's the right calibration, but I watch for when stubbornness is disguised as principle."

**Probe 4:** *"What was the hardest part of giving credit publicly?"*
→ "If I'm honest — ego. I had been the public advocate for the original position. Changing course publicly and attributing it to someone more junior required setting that aside. But the team culture payoff was immediate and lasting."

**Probe 5:** *"What would you have missed if you'd stayed wrong?"*
→ [Describe the specific downstream impact: architectural mistake avoided, customer value preserved, etc. Make it concrete.]

## Story 5 — Diverse Viewpoints

**Probe 1:** *"How do you get people to share their real views, not just agree with you?"*
→ "Ask for dissent explicitly: 'What's the strongest argument against this approach?' Separate idea generation from evaluation — no evaluating during the brainstorm. And follow up 1:1 with people who went quiet in the group session."

**Probe 2:** *"What do you do when you've genuinely incorporated diverse input but some people still feel unheard?"*
→ "Make the trade-offs transparent. 'Here's what we heard from each group. Here's what we optimized for and why. Here's what we explicitly traded away.' People can accept not getting what they wanted if they understand the reasoning and feel heard in the process."

**Probe 3:** *"Give me a specific example of a view that changed the outcome."*
→ [Have a concrete answer ready — one specific piece of input from one specific person that changed the recommendation or design. Generic answers here feel hollow.]

**Probe 4:** *"How do you handle a situation where a minority view is right but the majority disagrees?"*
→ "I protect minority views from being steamrolled by group dynamics. I evaluate them on their merits separately, then bring them forward with my own analysis if I think they're correct. A minority view being outvoted isn't the same as being wrong."

**Probe 5:** *"What does inclusion look like to you in a technical team?"*
→ "Everyone's technical contributions are evaluated on merit, not seniority. Junior engineers are heard in architecture reviews. Off-hours meetings aren't the place where real decisions happen. And when someone spots a problem, they feel safe saying it."

## Story 6 — CE Technical Communication

**Probe 1:** *"How do you know when you've lost the business audience?"*
→ "Body language and question type. If a business leader starts asking feature questions, they've lost the thread. If they go quiet, I've lost them. I explicitly check in: 'Does this level of detail help or should I go higher-level?'"

**Probe 2:** *"What do you do when the technical audience and business audience want completely different things from the same conversation?"*
→ "Separate them when possible — pre-read for the business person, technical appendix for the engineer. When they're in the same room, lead with the business outcome and offer to go deeper on the technical layer for those who want it. Never make the business leader feel out of depth."

**Probe 3:** *"Tell me about a time a customer said your technical solution was too complex and they couldn't adopt it."*
→ [Have a real story here. If this has never happened — what's your theory? How would you prevent it?]

**Probe 4:** *"How do you maintain technical credibility with a customer's engineering team while also advising their executives?"*
→ "Don't oversimplify for the engineers. When you're with them, go deep. When you're with executives, go broad. Make sure the engineers know you respect their depth — ask them to review your architecture choices, not just accept them."

**Probe 5:** *"What's the hardest customer communication you've had to make?"*
→ [Likely: delivering bad news — a timeline slip, a product limitation, a mistake your team made. Show you led with transparency, not spin.]
