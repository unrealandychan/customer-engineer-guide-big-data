# Interview C — Andy Interviewer Profile & Strategy

**Andy:** Platform Customer Engineer Team Manager  
**Interview:** 45-minute Googleyness & Leadership Round

---

## What Andy's Job Tells You About What He Values

Andy manages a team of **Platform Customer Engineers** — generalist/platform CEs who handle a broad customer portfolio. The role you're interviewing for is a **specialist CE** (Data Analytics), which is a *different* profile from his direct reports:
- His team: Platform CEs assigned to accounts, covering broad cloud adoption
- Your role: Specialist CE called in on the hardest, most specific data problems

### What This Means for Your Answers

1. **He understands CE life deeply** — he'll see through any answer that oversimplifies the job. Be real about the hard parts: ambiguity, dual audiences, customer politics.
2. **He's evaluating: "Would this person make my CEs look good?"** — A specialist CE either elevates the platform team or creates confusion. Show you know how to collaborate with generalist CEs, not compete with them.
3. **He'll test CE-specific leadership** — can you influence a customer's technical direction without authority? Can you navigate a deal where engineering says yes but procurement says no?
4. **He'll probe for Googleyness in concrete situations** — not abstract principles. "Tell me a time you..." not "do you believe in..."

---

## Andy's Probable Interview Approach

**Based on G&L guidelines and the role:**

He is likely to give you:
- **2 behavioral questions** (with deep follow-up probes)
- **1 "what makes a great/bad CE" or "why Google / this role" question**
- **0–1 light hypothetical** (people/ambiguity focused, not technical)

Each behavioral question will be followed by **3–5 probes**. He'll push until he finds a gap or confirms a strength.

**His scoring lens:**
- Googleyness: Does this person embody ownership, humility, bias for action, doing the right thing?
- Leadership: Have they stepped up WITHOUT authority? Did they make the situation better?
- CE-fit: Does their instinct match what great CEs do (customer-first, technically credible, translates both ways)?
- Cultural add: Will this person make Google better — not just fit in?

## The Questions Most Likely from Andy

### Tier 1 — Highest probability (practice cold)

**Q1: "Tell me about a time you handled trade-offs and ambiguity simultaneously."**
- Use Story 1A or 1B (Leading Without Authority) + ambiguity trigger phrase
- Key move: Name the ambiguity EXPLICITLY — "The requirements were unclear AND there was no single owner. Here's what I did about that..."
- Close with: "And I measured success by..." + what you learned

**Q2: "Tell me about a time you had an ethical dilemma or were pressured to cut corners."**
- Use Story 3 (Ethical Pressure) from `story-inventory.md`
- Key move: Name the VALUE you protected, not just the action you took
- Close with: "Was it the right call? Yes — here's why. What it cost me: [X]. I'd do it again because..."
- Andy will probe: "Would you do it differently?" → "No — same call. But I'd escalate earlier next time."

**Q3: "Tell me about a time you demonstrated leadership even though you weren't the formal manager."**
- Use Story 1A or 1B (Leading Without Authority)
- Key move: Make it clear you had NO formal authority — that's the point. Then show how you led anyway.
- "I didn't wait to be assigned. I stepped in because the gap existed and I was positioned to fill it."

### Tier 2 — Very likely (have framework ready)

**Q4: "What makes a great Customer Engineer? What makes a bad one?"**
- This is a culture and self-awareness question
- **Great CE:** Technical depth (goes hands-on, builds real things) + Customer empathy (understands their world, not just your product) + Translator (makes technical things land with business audiences) + Bias for building (POCs and prototypes, not just slide decks)
- **Bad CE:** Order-taker (executes requests without adding insight) OR brilliant-but-inaccessible (deep technical but can't make it land with a non-technical stakeholder)
- Self-aware add: "I tend toward the technical depth side — I'm always checking myself to make sure I'm not going too deep when the customer needs the 50,000-foot view."

**Q5: "Why Google? Why this specialist CE role specifically?"**
- Don't fake it. Be real and specific.
- Use this frame: "The specialist CE model is what draws me. I want to be the person who steps in on the most complex problems — the ones where a generalist solution breaks down. At Google scale, those problems are at the frontier of what's technically possible. And I'd be working alongside the engineers who built the products I'd be deploying. That's rare."
- Optional add: Name a specific Google product or capability that genuinely excites you (BigQuery + ML integration? Dataplex for governance? Vertex AI Pipelines?)

**Q6: "Tell me about your biggest failure — what happened?"**
- This is a Googleyness + Humility question — don't deflect
- Choose a real failure (not a humblebrags like "I worked too hard")
- Own it clearly: "My mistake was..." not "the situation was..."
- The Lessons line IS the answer here. Make it specific and forward-looking.
- Show you changed behavior, not just your thinking.

### Tier 3 — Possible backup

- "Tell me about a time you received difficult feedback."
- "How would you ensure your team is diverse and inclusive?" (leadership variant)
- "Tell me about a time you developed or retained a team member."
- "If you had coffee with Sundar Pichai, what would you talk about?"

## Andy's Follow-Up Probe Patterns

Andy is a manager — he coaches people for a living. He'll probe the human/relational layer that behavioral answers often gloss over.

| Probe | What He's Testing | Your Move |
|---|---|---|
| "How did others react?" | Emotional intelligence, team awareness | Name specific people's reactions — don't generalize |
| "Did the relationship survive?" | Maturity, long-term thinking | Show you preserved the relationship even through disagreement |
| "What was your manager's view?" | Ability to manage upward | Be honest — even if you disagreed with them |
| "What would you have done if they'd said no?" | Resilience, non-attachment to outcome | "I'd have committed to the decision while documenting my concern..." |
| "Was this the right hill to die on?" | Values clarity | Own your answer — "Yes, because [user/integrity/team]" |
| "What did this cost you?" | Ethical depth | If something doing the right thing costs you nothing, it's not a real values test |
| "How is that person/relationship today?" | Outcome and accountability | A good story ends with a maintained or stronger relationship |

## CE-Specific Probes Andy Will Use

Because he manages CEs, he'll dig into:

**"Tell me about a time you had to present the same technical concept to both an engineer and a VP in the same meeting."**
- Use Story 6 from `story-inventory.md` (CE Technical Communication)
- Key: Layered approach — business outcome in the title, technical rationale in the body, appendix for the engineer
- "I read the room — when the VP went quiet, I pulled back from the technical layer and asked 'is this level of detail helpful or should I go higher?'"

**"How do you build credibility with a customer's engineering team quickly?"**
- "I go deep before I go broad. When I first meet a customer's engineers, I ask about their architecture — I want to understand what they've built before I propose anything. People trust advisors who first understand, then advise."
- "I also show, not tell — running a quick POC or sharing a code sample says more than any slide."

**"Tell me about a time a customer rejected your technical recommendation."**
- Have a real example. Show you didn't take it personally.
- Show what you learned about the customer's real constraint that your recommendation didn't account for.

## The "Googleyness" Trap to Avoid

Andy will be watching for candidates who **perform Googleyness** vs. **demonstrate it**.

**Performing:** "I really value collaboration and always incorporate diverse perspectives." → Generic, unfalsifiable.

**Demonstrating:** "When we were designing the canonical schema, Brand C's engineer said something I initially dismissed — they pointed out our customer key design would break for loyalty program customers without a digital account. I stopped, re-examined, and they were right. I redesigned the key structure and credited them in the technical design doc." → Specific, concrete, Googley.

**Rule:** Every claim about a value should be followed immediately by a specific, named example.

## Andy-Specific Framing Strategy

### Open every answer with the context, briefly:
> "This was at [company type], where I was [role]. The situation was..."

Don't make him guess your context. But be brief — max 2 sentences.

### Use the "Values Protected" line explicitly:
At the end of every Story, say:
> "What I was protecting in this situation was [X] — because [why it mattered]."

This is Googleyness language. Andy will recognize it and it signals preparation without feeling scripted.

### After the Lessons line — add a behavior change:
Don't just say "I'd do X differently." Say:
> "...and since then, I've built [specific habit/practice/check] to make sure I catch this earlier. For example, [one concrete instance of applying the lesson]."

This shows the learning actually stuck — it's not just insight, it's changed behavior.
