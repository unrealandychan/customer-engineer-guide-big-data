## Story 7 — Received Difficult Feedback (Theme: Intellectual Humility + Growth)

**Theme:** You received feedback that was hard to hear — and you grew from it. Don't deflect. Own it.

**Why it matters:** Andy will probe for authentic humility. Google values people who update their behavior based on feedback, not just their thinking. If you "changed your mind" but not your actions — the Lessons line will reveal it.

**Backstory:**
> [Who gave the feedback? Your manager? A peer? A skip-level? A customer? What was the feedback — be specific. "You talk too much in meetings and it shuts down input from the more junior members." OR "Your written communication assumes too much technical knowledge from the audience." OR "You present solutions before you've fully understood the problem."]

**SPSIL:**

- **Situation:** ["I was [X months/years] into my role at [company]. I thought I was performing well — I had shipped [some recent win]. Then [person] said something I didn't expect."]

- **Problem:** ["The feedback was: [specific feedback verbatim or close to it]. My first reaction was honest discomfort — I didn't think that assessment was fair. But I sat with it for 24 hours before responding."]

- **Solution:**
  - Step 1: [I asked [person] for a specific example — not to argue, but to understand what they actually observed. "Can you show me the situation where it was most visible?"]
  - Step 2: [I did my own audit — I pulled three other people aside and asked: "I got some feedback that [X]. Does this match what you've observed?" The answers were uncomfortable. The feedback was right.]
  - Step 3: [I changed specific behaviors: [concrete behavior change]. Not "I tried to be more aware" — something observable. E.g., "I started writing a problem statement before proposing any solution in design docs." OR "I started asking the junior engineer to speak first in estimation meetings."]

- **Impact:** ["Within [timeframe], [specific observable change]. [Feedback-giver] later said [something showing the change landed]."]

- **Lessons:** ["The hardest part was the 24 hours between receiving the feedback and deciding how to respond. My instinct was defensive. What I learned is that my instinct to defend is itself a signal — if feedback makes me defensive, it's usually because it's true. Now I've made it a practice to wait 24 hours before responding to any critical feedback. The gap separates the ego from the analysis."]

**Follow-up probes Andy will ask:**
- "What was your first reaction internally?" → Be honest. "I was defensive. I didn't think it was fair." Then show how you moved past it.
- "What did you actually change — specifically?" → Have a concrete, observable behavior. Not "my mindset."
- "Did the relationship improve?" → Yes — and explain how doing the right hard thing usually strengthens relationships.

## Story 8 — Developing/Retaining a Team Member (Theme: Leadership + Investment in Others)

**Theme:** You helped someone grow, kept someone from leaving, or invested in someone's development in a concrete way.

**Why it matters:** This is more likely in Andy's round than Gene's (G&L assesses leadership broadly). Andy manages CEs — he's invested in people development. He wants to see you lead, not just perform.

**Backstory:**
> [Who was this person? A peer? A more junior engineer? A colleague in a different function? What was their situation — they were struggling, about to leave, lacking skills, being set up to fail? What did you step up to do without being asked?]

**SPSIL (pick ONE of the two subtypes below):**

---

### Subtype A: Helping Someone Develop a Skill

**SPSIL:**

- **Situation:** ["[Name/role] was [X months] into their role. They were technically solid but struggled to [communicate findings to non-technical stakeholders / run a customer meeting without over-engineering the discussion / structure written communication clearly]."]

- **Problem:** ["This was limiting their impact — they were doing great work that wasn't landing. And they were starting to feel frustration and disengagement because they weren't getting the visibility their work deserved."]

- **Solution:**
  - Step 1: ["I asked if I could give them some direct feedback. I framed it as: 'I think there's one specific thing that, if you worked on it, would 10x your visibility.' They said yes."]
  - Step 2: ["I offered to [pair on a document / sit in on their next customer call and debrief afterward / co-present the next project update and split the sections to show both styles]."]
  - Step 3: ["I gave feedback in real-time — not in a performance review context. After each session: 'This worked. This didn't. Here's why.'"]

- **Impact:** ["Within [timeframe]: [specific improvement — e.g., 'They gave their first solo executive briefing. The VP of Engineering asked my manager who they were — they'd never been on his radar before.' OR 'They became the default person on the team for customer communication.']"]

- **Lessons:** ["The most effective development feedback is specific and in-context. 'Be more concise' lands flat. 'In that last slide, you had 3 points where one was the real insight — which one was it?' lands differently. I learned to give feedback tied to a specific, recent moment, not a general trait."]

---

### Subtype B: Retaining Someone Who Was About to Leave

**SPSIL:**

- **Situation:** ["[Name] had been on the team for [X years] and was showing signs of disengagement — [details: quieter in meetings, stopped volunteering for new projects, I'd heard hints they were interviewing]."]

- **Problem:** ["I wasn't their manager — I had no authority to change their compensation, their title, or their assignment. And officially, I had no visibility into their situation. But I could see what was happening, and I didn't think it was right to wait for someone else to act."]

- **Solution:**
  - Step 1: ["I asked them to coffee with no agenda. I just said 'I want to catch up — I feel like we haven't connected in a while.' I didn't bring up what I was noticing."]
  - Step 2: ["When they opened up, they told me [what was wrong — unclear growth path, feeling overlooked, specific grievance]. I listened. I didn't immediately try to fix it."]
  - Step 3: ["I did what was in my power to do without overstepping: [specific action — 'I recommended them for the [project] lead role that was open, which gave them visibility to the director they hadn't had.' OR 'I connected them with [person] who had made the same career transition they wanted to make.' OR 'I gave them direct public credit for [piece of work] in the team meeting they'd been underrecognized for.'"]

- **Impact:** ["They [decided to stay / got the role / had a direct conversation with their manager that led to a change]. Eighteen months later, [outcome — still on the team, got promoted, led a key project]."]

- **Lessons:** ["You don't need authority to retain someone. You need to see them, tell them what you see, and do the specific thing you CAN do — not wait for the thing you can't do. The smallest genuine act of recognition at the right moment is worth more than a retention bonus that comes 6 months too late."]

## Story 9 — "What Would You Have Done if They Said No?" (Recurring Probe — Have This Ready)

This isn't a standalone question — it's a follow-up probe Andy uses on almost every story. Your answer reveals:
- Do you have conviction or are you people-pleasing?
- Can you commit to a decision even when overruled?
- Do you escalate when appropriate without going nuclear?

**Script for your answer (adapt per context):**

> "If they'd said no, I would have done a few things. First, I would have committed fully to their decision and executed it as though I'd made it myself — disagreeing and not committing is the worst outcome. Second, I would have documented my concern clearly in writing — not to cover myself, but to establish a shared understanding of the risk so that if the outcome was bad, we could learn from it rather than argue about it. And third, I would have set a specific trigger point to revisit: 'Let's evaluate this again at [6 weeks / after the Phase 1 launch / when we have the first quality report]. If the data shows [problem], I'd like us to reconsider.'
>
> I've learned that the goal of disagreeing is to be heard and to protect the outcome — not to win. And the way to stay heard is to disagree once, commit fully, and earn the right to revisit."

**The trap to avoid:** Don't say "I would have escalated to leadership." That sounds like you go above your manager's head on every disagreement. Only mention escalation if the situation genuinely warranted it (ethical violation, safety, legal issue).

## Story 10 — Navigating Competing Priorities / Multi-Tasking Under Pressure

**Theme:** You handled multiple demanding commitments simultaneously. You made a triage call. It was the right call.

**When it's asked:** GCA round (Gene, behavioral) — "Tell me about a time you had to manage competing priorities." OR G&L round (Andy) — as a follow-up to leadership under pressure.

**SPSIL:**

- **Situation:** ["I was simultaneously responsible for [Commitment A] with a hard deadline of [X] AND [Commitment B] which had just become urgent because of [trigger]. Both had executive visibility."]

- **Problem:** ["There wasn't enough capacity to execute both at full quality by their deadlines. Something had to give, but both stakeholders believed they had full priority."]

- **Solution:**
  - Step 1: [Triage — I assessed: which harm is worse if we miss? The answer was [A/B] because [specific reasoning — irreversible consequence, customer impact, revenue at risk]."]
  - Step 2: [Transparent communication — I went to [Stakeholder B] and said specifically: 'I need to tell you that [A] has a hard constraint I can't move. Here's what I can deliver on [B] by [date]. Here's what that means we defer.']
  - Step 3: [Scope negotiation — I presented an alternative: a reduced scope on [B] that delivered the core value without requiring full bandwidth. They accepted.]

- **Impact:** ["We delivered [A] on time at full quality. [B] delivered [specific reduced scope] on [date], which [Stakeholder] later said was more than they expected given the constraint."]

- **Lessons:** ["The temptation when priorities conflict is to quietly let something slip without telling anyone. I've learned that's almost always the wrong call — the loss of trust when the miss is discovered is worse than the discomfort of the early conversation. Delivering a smaller thing you committed to is better than breaking a commitment you didn't flag as at-risk."]
