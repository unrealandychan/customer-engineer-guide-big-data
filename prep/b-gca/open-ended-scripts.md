# Interview B & C — Open-Ended / Wildcard Scripts

These questions appear in BOTH the GCA round (Gene) and the G&L round (Andy).
Prepare scripted answers — but practice until they sound natural, NOT memorized.

---

## "Why Google? Why this role specifically?"

**When it comes:** Often at the END of a round as a wind-down question, but Gene or Andy might open with it.
**Target time:** 90 seconds maximum. Do not monologue.

**Script (adapt in your own voice):**

> "There are two parts to that answer — the company and the specific model.
>
> On Google: I've spent [X years] on the data platform side, and I keep finding myself at the boundary of what's technically possible. BigQuery's serverless architecture, the Beam unified model, Vertex AI's integration — these aren't just products I'd be demoing. They're solving problems that don't have clean solutions on any other stack. I want to work at the place that's defining the frontier, not following it.
>
> On the specialist CE model specifically: this role is the rare version of a CE that goes deeper, not broader. When a generalist solution breaks down — identity resolution across 3 schemas, a streaming pipeline that has to do both batch and real-time, an AI activation mandate with a 12-month board timeline — that's when a specialist gets pulled in. I want to be that person. I want to be the one who unblocks the deal by building the thing, not just presenting a slide about it.
>
> At Google's scale, those problems are happening at the frontier of what's technically possible. I'd be working alongside the engineers who built the products I'd be deploying. That combination is rare."

**Anti-patterns to avoid:**
- "I love Google's culture" → too generic
- "The compensation is great" → obvious, never say this
- "I've always wanted to work at a FAANG company" → sounds like a bucket list item, not a mission

## "Tell me about yourself"

**When it comes:** Often the actual OPENER for any round. Gene, Andy, and William may all ask this.
**Target time:** 60–90 seconds. It's a runway — don't waste it.

**Structure:**
1. **Current role** (1 sentence) — what you do
2. **Superpower** (1 sentence) — what you're uniquely good at
3. **The bridge** (1 sentence) — why this role is the logical next step
4. **Hook** (1 sentence) — what you want to show them in this conversation

**Script:**

> "I'm currently a [your title] at [company], where I spend most of my time at the intersection of data architecture and customer outcomes — specifically designing and building data platforms for [industry/customer type] that support both analytics and machine learning workloads.
>
> My superpower is translating between the engineering layer and the business layer. I can build the pipeline AND explain to a CIO why it matters for their board mandate. That's the specific skill set that makes a specialist CE effective.
>
> I'm here because I think Google Cloud's data stack — BigQuery, Dataflow, Vertex AI — is the most compelling answer to the problems I'm currently solving, and I want to be hands-on with it at the scale and complexity that only Google's customer base generates.
>
> I'd love to use today to show you how I think through the 3-brand merger scenario — both the technical architecture and the business case for why it changes the CIO's strategic position."

## "What's your favorite Google product, and why?"

**When it comes:** GCA round — Gene might ask this to warm up or to probe product passion.
**Target time:** 60 seconds.

**Answer:**

> "BigQuery — and specifically what it's become in the last 12 months as it's evolved toward an autonomous data-to-AI platform.
>
> What I find remarkable is that it's simultaneously a data warehouse, an ML training environment, a streaming analytics engine with continuous queries, and now a multimodal platform that can process images, documents, and structured data in a unified SQL interface. The fact that a data analyst can run an ARIMA or TimesFM demand forecast on production data without touching Python, without moving data, without provisioning a cluster — that's genuinely novel. No other platform has closed that loop.
>
> For the retail merger scenario I've been thinking about, BigQuery is the enabling technology for the whole 12-month AI activation mandate — not just the warehouse. The Gemini-assisted data canvas means brand analysts can self-serve without needing a data engineer for every question. That's how you scale AI activation across 3 brands in 12 months."

**Why this answer works:**
- Shows genuine technical depth, not just name-dropping
- Connects to YOUR presentation scenario (William's scenario)
- References a current product evolution — shows you stay current
- Focuses on the customer/business impact, not just the feature list

## "If you had coffee with Sundar Pichai, what would you talk about?"

**When it comes:** Andy's round (G&L) — this is a classic Googleyness/intellectual curiosity probe. Gene might also ask it.
**Target time:** 90 seconds. Don't be generic. Have a real point of view.

**What they're testing:** Do you have genuine intellectual curiosity? Can you engage with business strategy at an executive level?

**Script:**

> "I'd want to understand how Google thinks about the tension between openness and monetization in the cloud era. On one hand, BigQuery now natively supports Apache Iceberg and runs open Spark workloads — that's a deliberate bet on open standards. On the other hand, the Gemini-in-BigQuery flywheel only works if customers are deeply in the GCP ecosystem.
>
> That tension is real for every large enterprise I've worked with. The CIOs who are most committed to Google Cloud are often the ones who are MOST insistent on open standards — because they've been burned by lock-in before. And Google's answer to that seems to be: 'we'll be the best at open, so you choose us for capability, not because you can't leave.'
>
> I'd want to understand if that's a deliberate philosophy or an emergent outcome — and how he thinks about sustaining it as AI inference costs come down and cloud differentiation shifts toward data gravity rather than compute.
>
> I'd also ask him what he thinks a great CE does that no AI can replicate. Because that's the question I'm asking myself every day."

**Why this answer works:**
- Shows you understand Google's strategic positioning, not just its products
- Demonstrates intellectual curiosity at a business strategy level
- The last line creates a natural conversation hook ("Good question — here's what I think...")
- Not sycophantic ("I'd thank him for Google's mission"...)

## "What are your strengths and weaknesses?"

**When it comes:** Any round. More likely in G&L with Andy.
**Target time:** 75 seconds.

**Strength:**
> "My strongest skill is building technical credibility with customers quickly. I do it by going deep before going broad — when I first meet a customer's engineering team, I ask about their architecture before I propose anything. Within 20 minutes I can usually identify the one technical constraint that's being worked around rather than solved. That specific insight is what earns trust faster than any product demo."

**Weakness (real, with a growth arc — NOT a humblebrag):**
> "My weakness is impatience with process when I can see a faster path. In practice that sometimes means I start building before full alignment — which occasionally creates rework when requirements change. I've gotten much better at this by treating the first conversation as a design session, not a discovery session — I sketch an architecture on a whiteboard with the customer BEFORE I touch code. It forces alignment earlier. But I still have to consciously slow myself down when the path forward looks obvious."

**Why this structure works:**
- Strength is specific and shows the CE instinct (listen first, advise second)
- Weakness is real (not "I work too hard")
- Weakness has a concrete mitigation and an honest admission it's ongoing

## "Where do you see your career going?"

**When it comes:** Andy's round. Or as a follow-up to "Why Google."
**Target time:** 60 seconds.

**Script:**
> "I want to become the person who closes the most complex deals in data analytics — not by managing a team, but by being the technical depth the sales team calls when nothing else works. The specialist CE model at Google is the clearest path to that. In 3 years, I want to have 10 solved customer architectures in production and a pattern library that the broader CE team can learn from. That's what 'great' looks like to me for this role.
>
> After that — honestly — I'd be interested in contributing back to product. The CEs who've been in the hardest customer situations usually see failure modes that the product team doesn't. I'd love to eventually be that feedback loop."

## "What would you do in the first 30/60/90 days?"

**When it comes:** William's round (Presentation Q&A), or Andy's round.
**Target time:** 2 minutes spread — hit each phase clearly.

**Answer:**

**First 30 days — Listen and understand**
> "Listen more than I talk. I'd spend the first 30 days understanding: who are the top 5 active deals I'd be supporting, what are the recurring technical blockers, and what's the current CE playbook for data analytics? I want to understand what 'great' looks like on this team before I try to define it."

**Days 31–60 — Contribute and earn trust**
> "Start closing technical blockers on the active deals. At least one POC or demo that the sales team can use. Build a relationship with 2–3 of the platform CEs (Andy's team) so they understand how to engage me as a specialist — I want them to think of me as the person they call, not the person they compete with."

**Days 61–90 — Lead one technical deliverable end-to-end**
> "Own a customer technical evaluation from architecture design to delivered POC. The goal by day 90: one closed technical win I contributed to, and one internal artifact (architecture decision template, POC pattern) the team can reuse. That's the foundation for the first year."

**Closing line:**
> "The fastest way to earn trust in a new role is to deliver something real early. I'm not interested in spending the first quarter in ramp mode — I want to be useful immediately."

## "What makes a great CE? What makes a bad one?"

**When it comes:** Andy's round (directly) — and William may probe this in relation to how you'd operate in the role.
**Target time:** 75 seconds.

**Great CE:**
> "A great CE has three things in balance: technical depth (they build real things, not just describe them), customer empathy (they understand what the customer is actually trying to accomplish, not just what they asked for), and translation ability (they can make technical things land with a VP who doesn't want to hear about partitioning strategies).
>
> The 'great' differentiator, in my experience, is the bias for building. A great CE shows up with a working prototype, not a slide. The prototype does two things: it proves the architecture is real, and it gives the customer something to react to rather than imagine."

**Bad CE (two failure modes):**
> "Bad CE failure mode one: the order-taker. They execute exactly what the customer asks for, with no insight about whether it's the right thing. They optimize for customer happiness over customer outcomes.
>
> Bad CE failure mode two: the brilliant-but-inaccessible. Deep technical credibility, but they lose the business stakeholder in the first 5 minutes because they can't operate at two altitudes simultaneously.
>
> I tend toward the technical depth side of the spectrum — I have to consciously check myself to make sure I'm not going deeper than the conversation needs."
